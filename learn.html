Learn more about the rich tapestry of cultures that Yellowknife cherishes, including the Dene First Nation, North Slave Métis, Métis, and Inuit communities. It is worth noting that across wider North America, particulalrly in the Unites Staes of America to the south of Canada, there are many more First Nations peoples who are still seeking offical recognition from the state.

Here you will find a carefully curated catalogue of useful facts and interesting history. This information has been sourced from only credible official websites, reports, and a range of invaluable publications authored by members of First Nations and the voices of Indigenous communities themselves.

Learn about the key Indigenous groups in the Northwest Territories (NWT) and their contributions towards cultural preservation, governance, and UNDRIP implementation.

  • 1. Yellowknives Dene First Nation (YKDFN)
    Official Website: The YKDFN’s official site offers insights into their governance, community programs, and cultural heritage.
    Source: Yellowknives Dene First Nation Official Website
    Giant Mine Agreements: In August 2021, the YKDFN and the Government of Canada signed agreements addressing the historical impacts of the Giant Mine.
    Source: Government of Canada: Giant Mine Agreements
  • 2. Tłı̨chǫ Government
    Official Website: Provides comprehensive information on Tłı̨chǫ governance, culture, and community services.
    Source: Tłı̨chǫ Government Official Website
    UNDRIP Implementation: Instrumental in implementing UNDRIP within the NWT.
    Source: UNDRIP Implementation by Tłı̨chǫ Government

  • National Indigenous Peoples Day (June 21)
    Celebrated across Canada, this day recognizes and honors the heritage, cultures, and contributions of First Nations, Inuit, and Métis peoples.
    In the Northwest Territories: This day is a statutory holiday, making the NWT one of the few regions where it is officially recognized as such. Communities host events, feasts, traditional ceremonies, and cultural showcases.
  • Orange Shirt Day/National Day for Truth and Reconciliation (September 30)
    Established to honor the survivors of residential schools and their families while raising awareness about their legacy.
    Events include: Public commemorations, storytelling, and educational initiatives.
    In the NWT: Local Indigenous communities often hold ceremonies and events, and government offices and schools may participate in recognition.

  • Dene National Assembly (Dates Vary)
    An annual gathering of the Dene Nation, where leaders and community members come together to discuss issues affecting Dene communities. Often includes cultural events and ceremonies.
  • Tłı̨chǫ Annual Gathering (Dates Vary)
    Hosted by the Tłı̨chǫ Government, this gathering celebrates Tłı̨chǫ culture, governance, and heritage. Events may include hand games, drum dances, and community feasts.
  • Gwich’in Gathering (Every 2–3 Years)
    A cultural and governance event involving Gwich’in communities from the Northwest Territories, Yukon, and Alaska. It focuses on strengthening ties and discussing important regional matters.

  • Indigenous Cultural Festivals
    Many communities in the NWT host annual festivals celebrating their unique cultures. Examples include drum dances, storytelling sessions, and language workshops.
  • Northern Games and Competitions
    Events like the Dene Games and Arctic Winter Games celebrate Indigenous sports and traditions. These are often tied to regional celebrations.
  • Dene Hand Games Championships
    A cultural event showcasing traditional Dene games, held in various communities across the NWT.

  • Spring and Fall Hunting Camps
    Some Indigenous communities hold ceremonial events to mark traditional hunting seasons, celebrating their connection to the land and wildlife.
  • Seasonal Gatherings
    Ceremonies during solstices and equinoxes are important to many Indigenous cultures in the NWT, marking seasonal transitions with traditional practices.

  • Truth and Reconciliation Week
    Schools and community organizations often dedicate time leading up to September 30 to educate about residential schools and Indigenous history.
  • Indigenous Language Celebrations
    The NWT is home to 11 official languages, including nine Indigenous languages. Language events often occur throughout the year, celebrating preservation efforts.
